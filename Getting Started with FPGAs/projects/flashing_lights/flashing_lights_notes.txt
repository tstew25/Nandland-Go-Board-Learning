Notes:
The goal of this project was to create different patterns that the
LEDs would light up in based on what button was pressed. This wasn't
part of the Nandland book (as of Chapter 6, at least), it was just an 
idea I came up with on my own. 

As I'm learning a new language or topic, it's easy to simply follow along
the book or tutorials for making projects, but it's also good to try to create
your own and see it through. This was fairly straight forward, luckily, though
pattern 4 was definitely the hardest. Luckily all of the chapters of the book
thus far helped a lot, especially the Common FPGA Modules section. Referring back
to notes from that and previous chapters, along with the help of Google, reading
documentation, and AI here and there when I couldn't find an answer elsewise, I was 
able to figure out a working solution (which probably isn't the best, but that's the 
point of learning). 

In the end, once I remembered the pattern of an LFSR was exactly what I needed
for pattern 4, it then became an issue of figuring out how to get it to reverse
the LEDs once it got to a certain point. I tried many variations of this before
realizing I could simply check the MSB. This honestly happened because I was
trying to write a simulator for it since I couldn't get it to work and wasnted to
see where it was going wrong. Eventually I got a testbench of it working and 
saw that various hex numbers were being lit up in the sim (based on what bits
were being enabled at that clock cycle). That's when I just wrote out the 
bit patterns and the hex numbers I should expect to see for the pattern I wanted
and noticed that the MSB would be a good way to check when the pattern needed
to switch.

The next challenge was figuring out a good way to pick which module would 
be used based on the button input. I had written led_toggle.v to somewhat
simulate this by just having it light up an LED and remain lit until a new
button was pressed. Initially I tried to tie functionality like this into
an Enable bit that would be fed to the modules based on the button pressed.
After receiving warnings from Verilator that o_LED was being "multidriven"
and doing some light googling to determine what that meant, I stumbled across
a Reddit post with a similar question, to which someone mentioned that you
don't really need to control the input, just the output, since all modules
will still run across all clock cycles. 

So at this point I was basically using a demux to enable the modules and then
a mux to take the different outputs and send it to one output based on the 
button. But then after reading that comment, I tried removing the Enable bit
and just using a mux to send the wanted output to the LEDs and it worked all
while being simpler overall.

Still to do: there's probably still better ways to implement some of this, but
I'll learn as I keep going. I also still want to write a testbench for the 
project as a whole, but again, I'll do that when I get to it.
